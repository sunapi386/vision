# The Founder Story

I didn't plan to start a company. The path was supposed to be: get into the best CS program in Canada, get hired by the best companies in the world, build important things, and keep doing that until retirement. For ten years, that plan worked exactly as designed.

## University of Waterloo

UWaterloo's CS co-op program is a pipeline. You alternate between four months of school and four months of industry, and by the time you graduate, you've done six work terms at companies most people only read about in press releases. The program self-selects for a specific type of person: technically strong, professionally hungry, and comfortable being dropped into a new codebase every four months.

I also did an exchange semester at EPFL in Lausanne, Switzerland. Academically, it was rigorous. But the real education was environmental — being dropped into a research culture that valued deep, slow thinking over shipping velocity. European computer science asks different questions than North American computer science. They want to know why something works, not just that it works. That intellectual habit stuck with me.

## Apple — iPhone Hardware Reliability

My first role after graduation was at Apple, working on iPhone hardware reliability. Not software — hardware. The physical object that ships in hundreds of millions of units. At Apple's scale, a failure rate of 0.01% means tens of thousands of angry customers, front-page headlines, and congressional scrutiny.

Hardware reliability at Apple means understanding failure modes that span physics, materials science, manufacturing tolerances, and user behavior. You learn to think in distributions, not point estimates. You learn that "it works on my desk" is meaningless when your product ships to every climate zone on Earth. And you learn that the most expensive bugs are the ones you find after the production line is running.

The discipline that Apple instills — the obsession with getting things right before they ship, because you can't push an OTA update to fix cracked glass — shaped everything I've built since. Software people think in iterations. Hardware people think in consequences.

## Amazon Lab126 — Astro Robot

From Apple, I went to Amazon Lab126, the hardware R&D lab in Sunnyvale that builds Amazon's consumer devices. I worked on Astro, the home robot. My role spanned the full lifecycle: prototype to production.

Building a robot that navigates a home is fundamentally an AI problem. You're dealing with SLAM (simultaneous localization and mapping), object recognition, path planning, natural language understanding, and all of it running on edge compute with thermal and battery constraints. But the harder problem isn't the algorithms — it's the integration. Making all of these systems work together, reliably, in an environment you can't control (someone's living room), with users who haven't read a manual.

Lab126 taught me what production AI actually looks like. Not a research paper with a metrics table — a product that ships to real people and has to work when the WiFi drops, the lighting changes, and a toddler is standing in front of the sensor array. The gap between "state of the art on the benchmark" and "works in Karen's kitchen" is where most AI projects die. I learned to bridge that gap.

## AutoX — Seed to Series A

Then I joined AutoX, a self-driving car startup, as engineer number ten. When I arrived, there were ten of us in a single room. When I left, there were over 150 people, the company had raised its Series A, and we were running autonomous vehicles on public roads.

Joining a startup at the seed stage teaches you things that no large company ever will. You learn that resource constraints breed creativity — when you can't buy the answer, you have to build it. You learn that hiring is the single most important thing a startup does, because every early hire either multiplies or divides the team's output. You learn that speed matters more than perfection, but only up to a point — in self-driving, "move fast and break things" has literal consequences.

At AutoX, I went from being someone who builds components to someone who owns systems. That's the transition that matters in engineering: from "I wrote this module" to "I'm responsible for this car not killing anyone." The stakes sharpen your thinking in ways that no code review ever will.

The other thing AutoX taught me was how startups scale. At ten people, everyone knows everything. At fifty, you need process. At a hundred, you need management layers. At a hundred and fifty, you need culture, because you can't personally onboard every new hire anymore. Watching that evolution from the inside — and seeing which decisions accelerated it and which ones created drag — was the best education I could have gotten for eventually starting my own company.

## The Identity Shift

After a decade of this — Apple, Amazon, AutoX, each one bigger and harder and more complex — I had the resume. Senior engineer at the world's best companies, shipping production AI systems that touched millions of users. The playbook said: keep climbing. Principal engineer. Distinguished engineer. VP of Engineering. The path was clear.

Instead, I started a company.

In 2023, I founded AceTeam and Ada Nomad. The technical transition was straightforward — I'd been building AI systems for over a decade. The identity transition was the hard part.

When you're an engineer, your peer group is other engineers. You talk about architectures, frameworks, performance benchmarks. Your credibility comes from what you've shipped. When you become a founder, your demographics shift. Suddenly your peer group is decision-makers — executives, government officials, investors, university deans. You talk about markets, partnerships, runway. Your credibility comes from what you can convince other people to believe.

"My demographics shifted from other engineers to decision-makers." That's the most honest way I can describe it. I went from rooms where I was the most technical person to rooms where I was the most technical person but that wasn't the point anymore. The point was whether I could translate technical capability into business value, strategic partnerships, and institutional adoption.

That translation — from building things to building businesses and ecosystems — is the work I've been doing for three years. It's harder than any engineering problem I've faced. Systems are logical; people and organizations are not. Code compiles or it doesn't; partnerships develop on timelines measured in months and quarters, not sprints and releases.

But here's what I brought from the engineering side that most business people don't have: I know what's real. I know the difference between a demo and a product. I know which technical claims are credible and which ones are marketing. When someone tells me their AI does something, I can evaluate whether that's actually possible with current technology — because I've built the systems that sit at that frontier.

That's the unfair advantage of a technical founder selling to technical buyers. You can't bluff someone who's been in the trenches.
