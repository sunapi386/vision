# Bootstrapping Philosophy

There's a dominant narrative in tech: raise venture capital, hire aggressively, grow at all costs, figure out the business model later. This narrative has produced some spectacular outcomes — and an order of magnitude more spectacular failures. We chose a different path.

## The Numbers

AceTeam and Ada Nomad are bootstrapped. No external funding. No venture capital. No angels writing checks at demo day.

The burn rate is $25,000 per month. That covers a three-person team, infrastructure, and the unsexy operational costs that don't show up in pitch decks — accounting, legal, domain renewals, the occasional flight to present at a conference. Fifty months of personal runway means I can keep the lights on through 2027 without a single new dollar coming in.

Those numbers might sound modest. They are. That's the point.

## Consulting Funds Product

The model is simple: consulting revenue pays the bills while product development happens in parallel. We do real work for real organizations — deploying AI workflows, building integrations, standing up infrastructure — and we get paid for it. The product we're building is, in many ways, the distillation of what we learn doing that consulting work.

This isn't a pivot or a compromise. It's a strategy. Consulting keeps you close to the customer. When you're in the room with a police service trying to automate report processing, or a university professor trying to build an AI-augmented curriculum, you learn things that no amount of user research will tell you. You learn where the actual friction is. You learn which features matter and which ones you built because they seemed cool on a whiteboard.

The venture-backed version of this company would have raised $3 million, hired fifteen people, built for eighteen months in isolation, and then discovered that the product didn't match the market. We've watched that movie play out dozens of times. Instead, every feature we build has a customer who asked for it — and usually a customer who's already paying for a manual version of it.

## Discipline of Constraints

Constraints are clarifying. When you have unlimited budget, everything is a priority. When you have $25,000 a month, you make hard choices about what actually matters. We can't afford to build features nobody uses. We can't afford to chase trends. We can't afford to hire people who don't immediately contribute.

This discipline shows up in the product. Our stack is lean. We use Nix for reproducible builds because we can't afford to waste time debugging deployment environments. We use typed protocols because we can't afford runtime errors in production. We dogfood everything because we're our own most demanding user.

The constraint also shapes the team structure. We don't have a dedicated QA team, so every engineer writes tests. We don't have a DevOps team, so everyone understands the infrastructure. We don't have a product manager, so every decision is informed by direct customer contact. These aren't deficiencies — they're features of a team that's too small to hide behind specialization.

## The Honest Version

"Bank accounts are dry but the product is real."

I'll say what most founders won't: bootstrapping is hard in ways that have nothing to do with technology. It's hard because you're watching your savings account decline every month. It's hard because your peers at Big Tech companies are pulling in $400K total comp while you're deferring your own salary. It's hard because every "maybe" from a potential customer lands differently when your runway is finite.

The team operates on deferred pay and equity arrangements. Part-time commitments. Side hustle strategies. People take other jobs to cover their expenses while continuing to contribute to the product. This is the reality of building something from nothing without someone else's money — it requires the kind of commitment that a salary and stock options can't buy. You have to actually believe.

And here's the part that the hustle-culture narrative gets wrong: believing doesn't mean being delusional. It means being clear-eyed about the risks and deciding they're worth taking anyway. The product is real. The technology works. The customers exist. The question isn't whether what we've built has value — it's whether we can survive long enough for the market to catch up.

## Why Bootstrapping Matters

The deepest reason to bootstrap isn't financial discipline, though that helps. It's alignment.

When you take venture capital, you align with the investor's timeline. They need a return in seven to ten years. That means you need growth metrics that justify the next round, which means you optimize for the metrics that impress investors, which aren't always the metrics that serve customers. The incentive structure bends the company toward fundraising theater instead of customer value.

When you bootstrap, you align with the customer's timeline. They need a solution that works today. They don't care about your Series B strategy. They care about whether the workflow you promised them actually runs, whether the data stays on their infrastructure, whether you'll still be around in two years. Your survival depends on their satisfaction, not on a pitch deck.

This alignment produces a fundamentally different kind of product. Venture-backed products are designed to capture users and extract value — that's what growth metrics reward. Bootstrapped products are designed to solve problems and retain customers — because if they leave, you die. The product decisions that flow from "retain" are very different from the decisions that flow from "capture."

We build what customers actually need, not what investors think they want. That's not a slogan. It's a survival strategy.
