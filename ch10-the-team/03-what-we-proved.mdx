# What We Proved

The difference between a startup and a side project is evidence. Side projects have ideas. Startups have results. Here's what we've demonstrated — not on slides, but in production with real organizations making real decisions.

## Beat IBM and Deloitte on the Alberta RFP

In 2025, the Government of Alberta issued an RFP for an AI-powered workflow system. The incumbents — IBM and Deloitte — submitted proposals. So did we. Three people, no brand recognition, no government sales team, no lobbyists.

We won.

Not "we made it to the shortlist." Not "we got positive feedback." We beat IBM and Deloitte on a competitive government procurement. A three-person bootstrapped company, evaluated on the same criteria as firms with thousands of consultants and decades of government relationships.

This matters for two reasons. First, it proves the product is competitive at the highest level. Government procurement isn't a popularity contest — it's a structured evaluation with weighted criteria, reference checks, and technical assessments. The evaluators didn't know or care that we were small. They cared about what we could do.

Second, it shatters the assumption that you need scale to win enterprise deals. The conventional wisdom says small companies can't compete for government contracts. That's wrong. Small companies can't compete on marketing and relationship depth. But on technical merit, solution architecture, and value per dollar — a small team with the right technology can beat anyone.

## Three Police Services Piloting

We currently have three police services piloting our platform. Law enforcement is one of the most conservative, risk-averse institutional buyers in existence. They don't adopt technology because it's interesting — they adopt it because it solves operational problems that are costing them time, money, and public trust.

Police services generate enormous volumes of unstructured data: incident reports, witness statements, body camera transcripts, FOIP requests. Processing this data manually is expensive, slow, and error-prone. Our workflows automate the processing while keeping the data on the service's own infrastructure — which, in policing, isn't a nice-to-have. It's a legal requirement.

Getting three police services to pilot simultaneously is significant. These organizations don't talk to each other about vendor evaluations. Each one independently decided, through their own procurement process, that our platform was worth testing. That's three independent validations of the same value proposition.

## SJSU Partnership

San Jose State University partnered with us to run a three-session AI workshop series reaching approximately 150 students. This wasn't a sponsorship or a brand placement — it was a co-designed educational program where students used our platform to learn AI workflow automation.

The partnership with SJSU did something more important than generate leads: it validated our platform as an educational tool. When a professor puts your product in front of 150 students and builds a curriculum around it, that's a different class of endorsement than a customer testimonial. It means the product is intuitive enough for students to learn, powerful enough to teach real concepts, and reliable enough to survive a classroom full of people trying to break it simultaneously.

The SJSU relationship opened doors to the broader CSU system — 23 campuses, approximately 480,000 students. The opportunity to become the standard AI workflow platform for California's largest public university system is the kind of distribution channel that venture-backed companies spend millions trying to manufacture.

## McMaster Advisory Committee

McMaster University's DeGroote School of Business invited me to join their advisory committee for AI and entrepreneurship. This isn't a consulting engagement — it's a strategic relationship with one of Canada's top research universities.

Through McMaster, we're exploring an AI front-door pilot: an intelligent intake system for their entrepreneurship programs that routes students to the right resources, mentors, and programs based on their stage, sector, and needs. This is exactly the kind of workflow automation that our platform is designed for — complex routing decisions that currently require human judgment but follow patterns that AI can learn.

The McMaster relationship also connects us to the broader Ontario innovation ecosystem: government research programs, defense partnerships through IDEaS, and a network of researchers working on the same problems we're solving commercially.

## SPUR Innovation — $20M GPU Deployment

SPUR Innovation, a Canadian compute infrastructure company, deployed $20 million worth of GPU hardware. We're working with them on the workflow layer that makes that hardware useful — because raw compute without orchestration is just expensive electricity.

This partnership validates a core thesis of our platform: that the value in AI infrastructure isn't the hardware. It's the software layer that turns compute into outcomes. SPUR has the GPUs. We have the workflow engine that routes tasks to those GPUs, manages the execution, and delivers results. Together, we provide the complete stack — from silicon to solution.

## The Resume

These aren't slide deck claims. They're real engagements with real organizations that made real decisions to work with us. Let me put them in context:

| Engagement | What It Proves |
|-----------|---------------|
| Alberta RFP win over IBM/Deloitte | Product competes at enterprise level |
| Three police services piloting | Value proposition works in highest-trust environments |
| SJSU workshop partnership | Platform works as educational infrastructure |
| McMaster advisory committee | Strategic credibility with research institutions |
| SPUR $20M GPU deployment | Technology integrates with real compute infrastructure |

Each of these was earned, not bought. No sales team. No marketing budget. No brand campaigns. Every one of these relationships started with a conversation where someone described a problem, and we demonstrated — live, on their data, on their terms — that we could solve it.

There's a term in the enterprise AI world that captures what we do: forward-deployed engineering. Palantir popularized it. They send engineers directly to customer sites to solve problems in situ, with the product as the foundation and custom work as the bridge. That's exactly what our consulting model does — just without the brand name, the $50 billion market cap, and the hundreds of forward-deployed engineers.

We have three people doing the work that Palantir does with teams of twenty. The reason it works is the product. When the platform handles 80% of the workflow, you don't need a large team for the remaining 20%.
