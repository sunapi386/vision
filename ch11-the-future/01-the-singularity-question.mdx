# The Singularity Question

There's a moment coming — maybe in five years, maybe in fifteen — when AI surpasses human capability in most cognitive domains. Not all of them. Not the ones that require embodied experience, emotional intelligence, or the particular kind of pattern recognition that comes from being a biological organism navigating a physical world. But most of the domains that we currently pay people to perform: analysis, synthesis, writing, coding, planning, decision support, research, design.

This isn't speculation. It's trajectory. The rate of improvement in large language models, multimodal systems, and agentic architectures is not linear — it's compounding. Each generation of AI is being used to build the next generation. The tools that researchers use to write papers, design experiments, and analyze results are themselves AI systems. The feedback loop has closed.

The question isn't if. It's when. And more importantly: then what?

## What Does Work Look Like?

If an AI system can write a legal brief, analyze a medical image, generate a marketing campaign, debug a codebase, and draft a financial model — all at superhuman quality — what exactly are we paying knowledge workers to do?

The optimistic answer is: judgment. Humans become the editors, the validators, the people who decide which AI output to act on. Your job stops being "produce the analysis" and starts being "evaluate whether the analysis is trustworthy, contextualize it within institutional knowledge that the AI doesn't have, and make the call." The human becomes the last mile of quality assurance in a system that does everything else.

The pessimistic answer is: nothing. If the AI is also better at judgment, evaluation, and contextualization — and current trajectories suggest it will be, eventually — then the economic value of human cognition approaches zero for most professional functions. This doesn't mean humans stop being valuable as humans. It means humans stop being valuable as workers, in the narrow economic sense that our entire society is organized around.

The honest answer is: nobody knows. Anyone who tells you they have a clear picture of what the economy looks like after artificial general intelligence is either selling you something or deluding themselves. The range of possible outcomes is enormous — from post-scarcity utopia to concentrated oligarchic control to some messy, unpredictable middle ground that none of our current frameworks can describe.

## What Does Education Look Like?

"What are we doing with education when this is looming overhead?"

I ask this question in every conversation with university administrators, and it stops the room every time. Because the honest answer is uncomfortable.

We're currently training students for four years to acquire skills that AI systems are acquiring in four months. A computer science degree teaches you to write code — but code generation is rapidly approaching human-level quality. A law degree teaches you to research precedent and draft arguments — but legal AI is already being deployed in practice. An MBA teaches you to analyze markets and build financial models — but these are precisely the tasks that AI excels at.

The counterargument is that education isn't about skills — it's about thinking. Learning to think critically, to evaluate evidence, to construct arguments. That's true, and it's important. But it's also the argument that every disrupted institution makes right before the disruption: "what we really teach is something deeper than the surface skill." Maybe. But students are taking on $100,000 in debt to learn the surface skill, and they expect that surface skill to have economic value.

The universities that will thrive are the ones that reorient around what AI can't replace: the ability to ask the right questions (not find the right answers), the ability to exercise judgment in novel situations (not apply patterns from training data), and the ability to navigate the messy, ambiguous, politically charged reality of getting things done in organizations (not optimize a well-defined objective function).

This is exactly the educational model we piloted at SJSU and are developing at McMaster. Not "learn to use AI tools" — that's trivially easy and will be obsolete in two years. Instead: learn to work in a world where AI does most of the production work, and your value comes from direction-setting, quality assurance, and the kind of contextual understanding that comes from being embedded in a specific institution, community, or domain.

## The Ownership Question

Here's what I believe: the organizations and individuals who own their AI infrastructure will have options. Those who rent it won't.

When the singularity arrives — or even the pre-singularity period where AI is merely superhuman in specific domains — the question that matters is: who controls the AI? If your organization runs on AI systems hosted by a cloud provider, subject to their terms of service, their pricing, and their decisions about what the AI can and can't do — you're a tenant. Your options are limited to what the landlord permits.

If your organization runs on AI systems that you own — on hardware you control, with models you've trained or fine-tuned on your data, executing workflows you've designed — you have options. You can adapt as the technology evolves. You can make decisions that serve your interests, not the platform provider's interests. You can operate even if the cloud provider raises prices, changes policies, or goes offline.

This is the deepest argument for sovereign compute and on-premise AI infrastructure. It's not about cost optimization or data privacy, though those matter. It's about maintaining agency in a world where the most powerful technology in human history is controlled by a handful of companies.

The singularity, if it comes, won't arrive as a single dramatic event. It will arrive as a series of capability thresholds being crossed, one domain at a time. Each threshold will create new dependencies for organizations that don't own their AI stack. Each threshold will create new options for organizations that do.

We're building the infrastructure that gives organizations those options. Not because we can predict exactly what the singularity will look like — nobody can. But because we can predict that the organizations with the most agency will be the ones that fare best, whatever happens.
