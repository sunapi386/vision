# The AceTeam Vision

## Foreword

This document exists because I needed to write it. Not for investors — we don't have any. Not for a board — we don't have one. For clarity.

When you spend three years building a company, talking to mentors, pitching to government officials, debating architecture with your team at midnight, and explaining your vision to everyone from university deans to police chiefs to defense procurement officers — the ideas sharpen. But they stay scattered across conversations, slide decks, and late-night voice memos. At some point, you need to sit down and write the whole thing in one place, end to end, so you can see whether it actually holds together.

This is that document. It was born from conversations with mentors, advisors, and partners — not written in isolation. Tej Sandhu at McMaster, Jalal and Nitin at SJSU, Dom Cocco and Rino Bellavia at Forge, Pascal's research lab, the officers at three police services who explained their data problems over coffee, the government evaluators who told us why they picked us over IBM. Every chapter carries the fingerprints of people who challenged, refined, and pressure-tested these ideas. I wrote the words, but the thinking is collaborative.

Who is this for? Future team members who need to understand what they're joining and why. Partners evaluating whether sovereign AI infrastructure is a real opportunity or a slide deck fantasy. Customers deciding whether to trust a three-person company with their AI strategy. And, honestly, myself — because writing forces precision that thinking alone doesn't. If I can't explain it in writing, I don't understand it well enough to build it.

What should you take away? That AI infrastructure is undergoing a structural shift as significant as the internet, cloud computing, and mobile. That the organizations which own their AI capability will have fundamentally different options than those which rent it. That a small team with the right architecture can compete with — and beat — the largest companies in the world. And that we know exactly what we're betting on, what has to be true for those bets to pay off, and what happens if we're wrong.

This is a long document. It's meant to be read in order, but each chapter stands alone. Start wherever your questions are.

*Jason Sun*
*San Jose, May 2026*

## Table of Contents

### [Chapter 1: The Shift](./ch01-the-shift/index.mdx)

The pattern that repeats: every decade, a structural change rewires the economy. Internet, cloud, mobile — each one democratized access in the short term and concentrated power in the long term. AI is the next shift, but it changes something more fundamental than where work happens or how much infrastructure costs. It changes who does the work.

### [Chapter 2: The Problem](./ch02-the-problem/index.mdx)

Why current AI adoption is broken. Organizations are trapped between cloud providers who own the infrastructure, model providers who own the intelligence, and consultancies who charge millions to connect the two. The result: most organizations either can't adopt AI or adopt it in ways that create dangerous dependencies.

### [Chapter 3: Sovereignty](./ch03-sovereignty/index.mdx)

Data sovereignty isn't a compliance checkbox — it's a strategic imperative. Why organizations need AI infrastructure they own and control. The regulatory landscape pushing compute to the edge. What happens when your AI provider decides to change the terms.

### [Chapter 4: The Platform](./ch04-the-platform/index.mdx)

AceTeam's architecture: a sovereign compute fabric that runs on hardware you own. The workflow engine, the agent framework, the orchestration layer. How the pieces fit together and why they were designed this way.

### [Chapter 5: Workflows](./ch05-workflows/index.mdx)

Workflows as the unit of AI value. Not models, not prompts, not agents in isolation — complete, composable processes that turn AI capability into business outcomes. How workflows are built, tested, deployed, and shared.

### [Chapter 6: The Marketplace](./ch06-the-marketplace/index.mdx)

A marketplace for AI workflows: built once, deployed anywhere, composed into larger systems. How workflow creators capture value. How organizations discover and deploy solutions. Why the marketplace model creates network effects that a single-vendor platform cannot.

### [Chapter 7: The Protocols](./ch07-the-protocols/index.mdx)

MCP, A2A, and the open standards that make interoperability possible. Why protocols beat platforms in the long run. How AceTeam builds on open standards instead of proprietary lock-in. The historical pattern: TCP/IP, HTTP, SMTP — openness always wins.

### [Chapter 8: Economics](./ch08-economics/index.mdx)

The business model and unit economics. How consulting revenue funds product development. Pricing architecture. The cost structure of sovereign compute versus cloud AI. Why the economics improve as hardware costs decline.

### [Chapter 9: Go to Market](./ch09-go-to-market/index.mdx)

How a three-person company sells to governments, universities, and police services. The consulting-to-product pipeline. Geographic strategy across Canada and the United States. The partnerships and channels that scale distribution without scaling headcount.

### [Chapter 10: The Team and the Journey](./ch10-the-team/index.mdx)

How a three-person bootstrapped team built a platform that beat IBM. The founder's path from Apple and Amazon to startup founder. The discipline of building with constraints. The evidence: Alberta RFP, three police pilots, SJSU, McMaster, SPUR. The culture of open source, protocol-first thinking, and radical efficiency.

### [Chapter 11: The Future](./ch11-the-future/index.mdx)

Where this goes in five to ten years. The singularity question and what it means for work and education. Commodity AI appliances in every office. The million-to-one cost collapse transforming every knowledge profession. Distributed data centers instead of mega-installations. And the four bets we're making — what has to be true, what happens if we're right, and what happens if we're wrong.
