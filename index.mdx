# Agent Accountability: The Missing Infrastructure for the AI Economy

## Foreword

This started as an internal document for the company I founded. It ended up as something bigger.

When you spend three years building AI infrastructure — talking to police chiefs about paperwork that consumes 40% of an officer's shift, debating calibration methods with university researchers, explaining data sovereignty to government procurement officers, watching a three-person team beat IBM on a provincial RFP — the ideas sharpen. But they stay scattered across conversations, slide decks, and late-night voice memos. At some point, you need to sit down and write the whole thing in one place, end to end, to see whether it actually holds together.

It does. And the argument turned out to be bigger than any one company.

The thesis is simple: **AI agents are doing real work, at scale, right now — and the infrastructure to account for what they do doesn't exist.** Not the models. Not the chips. Not the frameworks. The receipts. The citations. The cost trees. The consent records. The governance enforcement. The calibrated confidence that tells a human where to look. Everything that makes the difference between a demo and an operation.

This gap is not new. Every major technological revolution produces an accountability layer — double-entry bookkeeping for commerce, SWIFT for international banking, TLS for the internet, container standards for software deployment. The accountability layer always comes second. It always outlasts the revolution that created the need for it.

The agent economy is waiting for its accountability layer. This document describes what that layer requires, why every force in the industry is converging on the same need, and what happens to organizations, people, and nations during the transition.

It was born from conversations with researchers, mentors, and practitioners — not written in isolation. Every chapter carries the fingerprints of people who challenged, refined, and pressure-tested these ideas. I wrote the words, but the thinking is collaborative.

*Jason Sun*
*May 2026*
