# Chapter 8: The Economics

The economic model that makes sovereign AI viable — from commodity compute to vertical value capture.

AI pricing today is designed to extract maximum value from every inference call. Per-token pricing is the cloud computing play all over again: make the unit cost look small, make the aggregate cost unpredictable, and capture the margin between what compute actually costs and what the customer will pay. Organizations that run AI at scale report 3-10x budget overruns. This is not a failure of planning. It is a feature of the pricing model.

The hardware economics tell a different story. GPU costs are dropping. NVIDIA's B300 delivers inference at a fraction of the per-token cost charged by cloud providers. For organizations with any meaningful volume, the crossover point — where owning hardware costs less than renting inference — is already here.

But owning hardware and selling commodity compute is not a viable business either. Data centers that sell raw compute compete on price, margins shrink to 3-5%, and the S&P 500 returns more with zero operational risk. The economics only work if you are not selling compute. You are selling work products — completed analyses, processed documents, answered questions — where the value to the customer is 10x to 100x the cost of the underlying compute.

This chapter traces the economic logic from the pricing trap through the ownership crossover to the vertical value capture model that makes the whole system work.
