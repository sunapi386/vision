# The Public Sector

## Why Government Is the Ideal Early Buyer

Government is the customer profile that every enterprise AI company claims to want and few know how to serve. The procurement is slow. The compliance requirements are demanding. The budget cycles are rigid. The decision-making involves more stakeholders than any private-sector deal.

All of this is true, and all of it works in our favor.

Government agencies have three characteristics that make them ideal early adopters of sovereign AI infrastructure. Each one is a structural advantage, not a concession.

**They have budget.** Federal and provincial/state governments allocate billions to technology modernization. The Canadian federal government committed $700 million to an AI Compute Challenge and $890 million to Sovereign Compute infrastructure. The U.S. federal government has multiple AI-focused procurement vehicles across defense, intelligence, and civilian agencies. These are not speculative budgets waiting for an ROI case. They are allocated funds looking for qualified vendors.

**They have compliance requirements.** Government data is classified, regulated, and subject to residency requirements by default. The compliance burden that makes most AI vendors walk away from government deals is precisely the value proposition for sovereign AI. AEP's governance layer — data classification, consent checking at every boundary, audit trails — maps directly to government compliance frameworks. We are not trying to convince government buyers that they need compliance. We are showing them that our product was built for it.

**They have compute sitting idle.** Government agencies operate data centers. Provincial governments have computing infrastructure distributed across dozens of facilities. Federal departments maintain classified computing environments that cannot touch public cloud. This infrastructure often runs at 30-50% utilization. It is capital that has been deployed but is not generating value. The sovereign compute model turns that idle infrastructure into AI capability without sending data to external providers.

## The Alberta Story

Our highest-profile government engagement to date is also our most instructive cautionary tale.

Alberta issued an RFP for AI infrastructure. The scope: provincial-scale AI capabilities for government services. The competition: IBM, Deloitte, and 75 other vendors. We submitted. We were selected as a finalist. We beat IBM. We beat Deloitte. We beat the established government IT vendors with decades of provincial relationships and armies of proposal writers.

Then the RFP was cancelled at the eleventh hour.

The stated reasons were bureaucratic — reorganization, scope changes, budget reallocation. Our suspicion — shared by several other finalists — was that the procurement office used the RFP process to collect detailed technical proposals from 77 vendors, extract implementation strategies and architecture recommendations, and inform an in-house approach. Whether or not the RFP was ever intended to result in a contract is unknowable. The effect was the same: months of work, a strong technical showing, and no contract.

This is a pattern, not an anomaly. Government procurement processes routinely function as intelligence-gathering exercises. The organizations that survive in government sales are the ones that account for this in their strategy: participate in RFPs because the process builds relationships and visibility, but do not bet the company on any single procurement outcome.

The Alberta experience produced three tangible assets despite the cancelled contract: credibility (we can say "we beat IBM and Deloitte in a competitive procurement"), intelligence (we understand what provincial AI infrastructure requirements look like at scale), and relationships (the procurement officers and technical evaluators now know our capabilities). These assets compound in subsequent government sales conversations.

## Active Government Channels

The public sector strategy runs through multiple channels simultaneously because government procurement is slow and unpredictable. Diversification is not a luxury. It is a survival requirement.

**GovAI Coalition (U.S.).** A coalition of government-focused AI companies sharing procurement intelligence, coordinating policy advocacy, and jointly pursuing large-scale federal contracts. The value: access to procurement vehicles that individual companies cannot access alone, and shared intelligence about upcoming RFPs, budget allocations, and agency priorities.

**AccelerateGov (Canada).** The Canadian equivalent — connecting AI companies with federal and provincial government buyers. Canada's government AI procurement is less mature than the U.S. market, which is an advantage: fewer established vendors, less entrenched competition, and agencies that are actively seeking new approaches rather than renewing incumbent contracts.

**Municipal pilots.** City-level government is faster than federal or provincial procurement and more willing to experiment. Three police services are currently piloting AI-powered report writing. The use case is precise: officers spend hours writing incident reports that are 80% formulaic. An AI workflow that drafts the report from structured input — type of incident, parties involved, timeline, evidence — saves 45-90 minutes per report. At 20 reports per officer per week across a 500-officer department, that is 10,000 hours saved per week.

Municipal pilots matter because they generate visible, quantifiable outcomes that resonate up the procurement chain. A police chief who says "this tool saves my officers 10,000 hours per week" carries more weight in a provincial procurement conversation than any vendor's slide deck.

**IDEaS CFP (Canadian Department of National Defence).** The Innovation for Defence Excellence and Security program, with up to $1 million in non-repayable funding for the "Cognition and Trust" call. This is a research-aligned opportunity: the protocol's calibrated confidence scoring and provenance chains map directly to the DND's stated need for trustworthy AI decision-support systems. The academic research partnerships — University of Waterloo on calibration, McMaster on applied AI — strengthen the application.

## The Government Sales Cycle

Government procurement follows a pattern that is frustrating but learnable.

**Phase 1: Awareness (6-12 months).** The agency learns that you exist. This happens through conferences, through coalition membership, through contractor registrations (SAM.gov in the U.S., CanadaBuys in Canada), and through introductions from existing government contacts. You cannot skip this phase. Cold outbound to government agencies does not work. They buy from vendors they already know.

**Phase 2: Pilot (3-6 months).** A small-scale proof of concept, often funded by an innovation budget or a discretionary allocation. The pilot proves technical feasibility and generates the performance data needed for a full procurement. Pilots are low-revenue ($20K-100K) but high-signal. A successful pilot is the strongest possible evidence in a subsequent RFP response.

**Phase 3: Procurement (6-18 months).** The formal RFP-bid-evaluate-award cycle. This is where most vendors focus their effort. But the outcome is largely determined in Phases 1 and 2: the agency already knows who they want to work with, and the RFP is structured (consciously or unconsciously) to favor that vendor's capabilities. The vendors who win RFPs are the ones who shaped the requirements during the awareness and pilot phases.

**Phase 4: Scale (12-36 months).** Contract expansion from the initial department to adjacent departments, from the initial agency to peer agencies, from the pilot scope to production scope. Government contracts expand through internal referral, not through sales calls. A successful deployment in one department generates interest from adjacent departments. The contract grows organically.

Total cycle from first contact to scaled deployment: 24-48 months. This is why government cannot be the only channel. But it is why government is the highest-leverage channel over a 5-year horizon. The contracts are large, the retention is high, and the competition is thin once you are established.

## Data Sovereignty as a Wedge

The sovereign compute architecture is a wedge into government procurement that no cloud-dependent AI vendor can match.

When a government agency evaluates AI solutions, one of the first questions is: "Where does our data go?" For cloud AI providers, the answer is: to our servers, in our data centers, under our jurisdiction. For classified data, defense data, or data subject to residency requirements, this answer is disqualifying.

Our answer: "Your data stays on your infrastructure. The models run on your hardware. Nothing leaves your network." This is not a workaround. It is the architecture. The Sovereign Compute Fabric was designed to run on government infrastructure from the beginning.

This architectural advantage becomes a procurement advantage because it removes the biggest objection in every government AI evaluation. The security review that takes six months for a cloud-based solution takes two weeks for a solution that runs on the agency's own infrastructure. The data classification review that requires special handling for PHI/PCI/classified data is simplified when the data never leaves the classified environment.

Data sovereignty is not just a technical feature. It is the reason government will buy from us instead of from the cloud AI providers who would otherwise dominate on brand recognition and market presence.
