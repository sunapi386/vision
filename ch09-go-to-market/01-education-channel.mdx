# The Education Channel

## The Workshop Model

In March 2026, we ran a three-session workshop series at San Jose State University with 150+ business and MBA students. The format: first session introduced AI concepts through the lens of a real business task — resume screening. Second session had students build the workflow themselves using the platform. Third session discussed implications, limitations, and what this means for their careers.

The workshops generate leads. Fifty-plus per workshop, easily. Students are engaged, they sign up for accounts, they tell their friends. The post-workshop survey data shows genuine learning outcomes. The assessment data — pre-workshop and post-workshop — demonstrates measurable improvement in AI literacy and practical skill.

Here is the problem with those leads: students are not buyers.

A second-year MBA student is interested in AI. They may even be sophisticated about it after three workshop sessions. But they do not have budget authority. They do not have repeatable professional tasks that AI can automate. They do not have the organizational context to evaluate whether a platform solves a real problem. They have enthusiasm, not purchasing power.

This sounds like a criticism. It is not. The education channel does not exist to generate direct revenue. It exists to build three things that are much harder to buy: credibility, awareness, and a pipeline of people who will eventually be buyers.

## The Real Target

The person we are actually looking for in the education channel is not the student. It is the professional who is IN a business, who has been doing the same grunt work for three years, and who immediately recognizes the pattern when they see a workflow automate it.

This person already knows their problem. They know that processing 200 resumes for every open position takes 12 hours. They know that the monthly compliance report is 80% copy-paste from five different systems. They know that the customer onboarding workflow has six manual steps that could be three automated steps and one human review. They do not need to be convinced that AI is useful. They need to see a tool that solves their specific problem.

These people show up in education contexts — as MBA students returning to school mid-career, as professionals attending industry conferences, as corporate training participants sent by their employers. They are in the audience alongside the traditional students. And they self-identify quickly. The student asks "what models does this support?" The professional asks "can I connect this to our Salesforce instance?"

The education channel is the filter. It separates the curious from the committed, the students from the practitioners, the people who want to learn about AI from the people who want to use AI to solve a problem they already have.

## Scaling Through University Systems

SJSU was the starting point. The scaling path goes through the California State University system: 23 campuses, approximately 480,000 students, and a collective purchasing infrastructure that means winning one campus can lead to winning the system.

The specific entry points:

**The Chancellor's Office.** A $10,000 grant for replicable technology curriculum — small money, but it signals institutional endorsement. Once the curriculum exists in the Chancellor's framework, any campus can adopt it without individual procurement. The leverage is not the grant money. It is the distribution channel.

**The Applied Technology Expo.** Cross-campus demonstrations that expose the platform to faculty and administrators from all 23 campuses simultaneously. One well-received demo at an AT Expo can generate 5-10 campus partnerships.

**Faculty partnerships.** Professor Nitin Aggarwal at SJSU — 18 years in the Information Systems department, deep knowledge of CSU procurement, co-founder of an edtech startup — proposed the CSU system-wide scaling strategy. Nathan, a full-time MBA faculty member, is integrating the platform into his BUS 290 and 202 courses for summer 2026. Each faculty partnership anchors the platform in a specific department, which generates cross-department referrals within the university.

The positioning is important. We are not trying to replace existing CSU AI literacy courses. Those courses teach theory, ethics, and concepts. We are the "applied practitioner" tier: here is how you actually build and deploy an AI workflow to solve a business problem. The courses teach what AI is. We teach what AI does. Complementary, not competitive.

## McMaster and the Canadian Track

In Canada, the education channel runs through McMaster University in Hamilton, Ontario. The relationship is different from SJSU — deeper, more institutionally embedded, and connected to a broader ecosystem play.

McMaster's Associate Dean, Manaf Zargoush, is building an AI hub and developing new programs focused on applied AI. The AI Advisory Committee — which we have been invited to participate in — sets strategic direction for AI integration across the university. This is not a workshop series. It is an institutional relationship that positions AceTeam as infrastructure for how McMaster approaches AI education and research.

The McMaster relationship connects to several parallel opportunities:

**Research collaboration.** The University of Waterloo's calibrated confidence research — the 51-judge ensemble that achieved 0.75 AUC — feeds directly into the AEP protocol. The IDEaS CFP (Canadian Department of National Defence, up to $1M non-repayable) is a potential research funding vehicle. Academic partnerships produce both publishable research and protocol improvements.

**The Hamilton ecosystem.** McMaster is the anchor institution in Hamilton's technology ecosystem. The university's endorsement opens doors to the city's economic development office, the innovation factory, the startup community, and the municipal government. Education at McMaster is not just education. It is market entry into the Hamilton ecosystem.

**Upper Bound Conference.** The annual conference in Edmonton — where we are presenting in May 2026 — is the Canadian AI community's gathering point. Conference presentations build credibility with the exact audience that matters: researchers, founders, government officials, and enterprise AI leaders who understand the landscape.

## What the Education Channel Actually Costs

Education is a loss leader. The economics are clear-eyed.

Direct costs per workshop series: approximately $2,000-5,000 in preparation time, travel, and materials. Revenue generated directly from workshops: zero. Students do not pay. The university does not pay (or pays a nominal honorarium that does not cover costs).

What $5,000 buys: 150 accounts created on the platform. 50+ leads captured with contact information and engagement data. 3-5 faculty relationships that generate follow-on workshops, course integrations, and institutional referrals. 1-2 media mentions or case studies that are reusable in sales conversations with enterprises.

The ROI is not measured in direct revenue. It is measured in pipeline acceleration. A consulting prospect who has heard about AceTeam through an SJSU workshop, or through a conference presentation, or through a faculty referral, converts faster and at higher confidence than a cold outbound prospect. The education channel warms the pipeline. The consulting channel closes it.

## The Limitation and the Lesson

The education channel has a structural limitation: it is slow. University partnerships take 6-12 months to formalize. Curriculum integration takes a semester cycle. Conference speaking slots are competitive and scheduled a year in advance. The pipeline warming effect is real but gradual.

This means the education channel cannot sustain the company alone. It builds the foundation, but it does not pay the bills. That is what the consulting channel does.

The lesson from two years of running education workshops is this: the education channel's highest-value output is not the students it trains. It is the stories it generates. "We taught 150 SJSU students to build AI workflows, and here is the assessment data showing measurable outcomes" is a story that resonates with enterprise buyers, government procurement officers, and institutional partners. The workshop itself is the product. The story about the workshop is the marketing.
